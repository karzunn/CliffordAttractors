CLIFFORD ATTRACTORS

Imagine you are at a water park, and there is a very long and twisting waterslide. This is an unusual waterslide that starts where it ends, and you simply enter it from the side to ride the loop. The waterslide is a half pipe, whose walls extend up and out a huge distance. You can start the waterslide from just about anywhere inside its walls, slide down and eventually enter the loop.

This is essentially the same concept as a Clifford Attractor, and attractors in general. No matter where you start, you eventually fall into the loop and follow a set path. In this analogy, the waterslide is actually a few mathematical equations working together. The equations that create Clifford Attractors look like this:

Xn+1 = sin(A⋅Yn) + C⋅cos(A⋅Xn)

Yn+1 = sin(B⋅Xn) + D⋅cos(B⋅Yn)

To plot a Clifford Attractor, you start by picking a value of x and y. Simply solve the equations, and the resulting x and y values get fed back into the same equations again. This is done thousands of times, and by the end of it, you have thousands of x and y values. If you plot all of these values as pixel values on a canvas, you will see your Clifford Attractor. The values A, B, C and D are just constants. You can choose them to be whatever you want, and they essentially change the shape and path of the "waterslide".
